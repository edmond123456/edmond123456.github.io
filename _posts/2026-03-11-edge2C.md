To wrap up my updates for the **IEEE Haptics Symposium 2026**, I am excited to share our third accepted paper. This study dives into the relationship between how we move and how we feel—specifically, how exploration strategies impact our ability to distinguish tactile frequencies.

<!-- ## The Active Touch Challenge
Touch is fundamentally active. We move our hands to gather information about the world, but this movement comes with a sensory cost. A phenomenon called **tactile suppression** (or gating) often mutes our sensitivity during voluntary action. We wanted to see if constraining how a user moves could bypass this "muting" and lead to more accurate perception.



## Our Experiment: The PinArray Workspace
Using our custom-built **PinArray** device (a $3 \times 4$ grid of independently vibrating pins), we asked 21 participants to compare two sequential vibrations. We tested three different ways of exploring the 2D workspace:

1.  **Horizontal Sweeps:** Movement restricted to the medial-lateral axis.
2.  **Vertical Sweeps:** Movement restricted to the proximal-distal axis.
3.  **Free Exploration:** Participants could move in any direction using any strategy they chose.

We also looked at how the spatial distance between the two stimuli (40, 80, or 120 mm) affected their judgments.



## Key Findings: Why "Less is More"
Our results revealed that the way we organize our movements significantly shapes the quality of the information we acquire:

* **Constraints Lead to Accuracy:** Participants were much more accurate when their movement was restricted to a single axis (Horizontal or Vertical) than when they moved freely. Constrained scanning regularizes the timing and speed of contact, providing a more stable "sample" for the brain.
* **The Cost of Proximity:** Discrimination was most difficult at the shortest distance (40 mm). When stimuli are close together, the short time interval between them can cause "temporal masking," where one sensation interferes with the memory of the other.
* **The Vertical Advantage:** Even in "Free" exploration, the most successful participants spontaneously chose to use vertical sweeps. It turns out that simple, predictable paths are the most effective "exploratory procedures" for frequency tasks.



## Practical Takeaways for Haptic Design
This research provides clear guidelines for anyone designing touch-based interfaces or VR controllers:
1.  **Guide the User:** To help users perceive fine details (like frequency or texture), use physical or software-based "scaffolding" to encourage structured, single-axis movements.
2.  **Space Things Out:** Ensure haptic cues are separated by enough distance or time to avoid the "masking" effects we observed at short ranges.
