I am excited to share that our paper, **"Thermal and Tactile Integration in Human Liquid Perception Using Viscous Solutions and Visible Light,"** has been accepted for the **IEEE Haptics Symposium 2026**! 

This research explores a fundamental question: How does our brain decide that something is a "liquid," and can we systematically manipulate that perception?

## The Mystery of Liquid Perception
Humans do not have dedicated "wetness" receptors. Instead, the perception of liquid emerges from the integration of thermal and tactile cues. While previous research often focused on creating the illusion of liquid *without* actual moisture, our work takes the opposite approach: **Can we make a real liquid medium feel less like liquid?**

## Our Approach: Hydroptical Thermal Feedback
To test this, we utilized a **hydroptical thermal display** to precisely control skin temperature while the hand was submerged in liquid. By using visible light—which passes through water with minimal absorption—we selectively warmed the skin surface without altering the temperature of the surrounding solution.

We manipulated two primary variables:
1. **Thermal Input:** Adjusting light intensity (100, 200, and 300 klux) to stimulate warm-sensitive C fibers.
2. **Tactile Input:** Using viscous xanthan gum solutions (1%, 2%, and 3%) to minimize tactile stimulation.

## Key Findings
Through a series of psychophysical experiments, we discovered that liquid perception is a remarkably flexible, multisensory construct:

* **Warmth Reduces Liquidity:** Increasing light intensity consistently reduced the perceived sensation of liquid. Some participants even reported they could no longer feel the liquid surrounding their fingers when receiving warm inputs.
* **Viscosity's Impact:** Higher solution viscosity decreased liquid perception by reducing the dynamic tactile cues essential for the sensation.
* **The "Brain Delay":** We found a temporal lag of approximately **2.5 seconds** before liquid perception began to drop after the light turned on. This highlights that liquid perception is a complex, interpretive cognitive process.
