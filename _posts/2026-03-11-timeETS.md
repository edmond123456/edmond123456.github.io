Hot on the heels of our work on liquid perception, I’m thrilled to share a second paper accepted for the **IEEE Haptics Symposium 2026**: **"Temporal Integration Underlying Extrapolation of Thermal Sensation."**

This study investigates a "temporal puzzle" in human touch: why do we sometimes perceive warmth where there is only cold?

<!-- ## What is Extrapolation of Thermal Sensation (ETS)?
ETS is a curious phenomenon where perceived warmth spatially spreads beyond its physical boundary when a warm stimulator is touched just before a cold one. While we’ve previously modeled the *spatial* aspects of this spread, the *temporal* "why" remained a mystery—especially since warm and cold signals travel to the brain at vastly different speeds.


## The Conduction Velocity Puzzle
Warmth is carried by slow **C fibers** ($0.2–2.8 \text{ m/s}$), while cold is carried by much faster **Aδ fibers** ($3–36 \text{ m/s}$). This creates a fundamental problem: even if you touch a warm object first, the "cold" signal might actually reach your spinal cord before the "warm" one! 

We tested two competing ideas to see how the brain handles this:
1.  **Timestamp Priority (H1):** The brain cares only about which signal arrives at the spinal cord first.
2.  **Duration Priority (H2):** The brain integrates the signals over time; the "winner" of this temporal summation determines what you feel.

## Our Experiments
Using a custom-built thermal display with Peltier devices and precise PI control, we manipulated both the arrival order and the duration of thermal stimuli.


### Key Findings:
* **Duration is King:** We found that ETS is evoked as long as the warm stimulus lasts long enough, **regardless of the arrival order**. This robustly supports the **Duration Priority Hypothesis**.
* **The 3.6-Second Threshold:** When warm and cold stimuli are applied simultaneously, participants initially feel cold. However, after approximately **3.6 seconds**, the sensation reliably shifts to warmth.
* **A Dynamic Model:** This proves that the neurons in our spinal cord (Wide Dynamic Range neurons) aren't just spatial integrators—they are involved in a temporal competition.


## Engineering Better Haptic Interfaces
For designers of thermal displays, this research offers a clear guideline: to bypass the initial "cold" sensation and deliver immediate warmth, use a **"warm priming" strategy**. By activating warm actuators roughly 3.6 seconds early, you can ensure the user perceives the intended thermal spread instantly.
